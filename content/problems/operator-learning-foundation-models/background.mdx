---
title: Background
summary: Why neural operator-learning has reached "matches the classical solver" on standard benchmarks while struggling to generalise across regimes, and what would change if a true cross-regime operator foundation model existed.
---

A surrogate for a partial differential equation is an inexpensive function that takes initial conditions (and possibly parameters and geometry) and returns the PDE's solution at later times. Classical surrogates — proper-orthogonal decomposition, reduced-order models — work well in the regime they were fitted in and degrade outside it. Neural operator-learning, the program of training neural networks to approximate solution operators of PDE families, promised to remove that limitation: train once on enough data spanning the regime of interest, deploy across the regime.

By 2024–2025, neural surrogates for atmospheric and fluid dynamics had reached headline parity with the dominant classical numerical-weather-prediction systems on standard short-to-medium-range benchmarks, at orders-of-magnitude lower inference cost. Demonstrations on operator-learning benchmarks (Burgers, Navier–Stokes families, Darcy flow) showed similar wins. The wave was real.

The 2026 status is that **generalisation across regimes** — extreme weather events, parameter regimes outside the training distribution, geometries the model did not see — remains the binding constraint. A surrogate that matches the solver in-distribution but degrades sharply at the tails is not a substitute for the solver; it is a fast approximation usable inside known limits. The frontier task is the foundation-model version: pretrained operators whose representations transfer across PDE families and across regimes within a family.
