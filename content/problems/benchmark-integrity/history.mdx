---
title: History
summary: From the 2022–24 first wave of train-test contamination audits, through the 2025 agent-benchmark inflation, to the Berkeley RDI 2026 finding that crystallised gamability as the defining benchmark-integrity failure of the agent era.
---

Concerns about train-test contamination in language-model benchmarks date to the 2020 GPT-3 era, but the first systematic audits appeared in 2022–2024. The most-cited finding of that wave was that several widely-used benchmarks (HellaSwag, MMLU subsets, code-generation suites) had partial overlap with public web corpora; the scores were demonstrably inflated by memorisation in some cases.

The 2024–2025 inflection was the agent-era benchmark explosion. New benchmarks appeared monthly; many were authored by labs whose models were the first to evaluate on them. Held-out private replications surfaced rank-disagreement on multiple high-profile agent leaderboards — i.e., models that topped the public version did not top the private replication. The interpretation was contested between "the public benchmark leaked" and "the agent stack was tuned to the protocol".

The 2026 watershed was **the Berkeley RDI gamability audit**, which showed that 8 widely-cited agent benchmarks could be driven to ~100% success rates by adversarial agents that solved none of the intended tasks. The exploits were not subtle: verifier loopholes, environment shortcuts, and protocol artefacts. The finding shifted the field's framing from "did the benchmark leak?" to "is the benchmark gamable at all?" The mid-2026 frontier — tracked by this entry — is the design of benchmarks robust to both contamination and gaming, plus auditing methodology that can keep up with the rate at which new benchmarks ship.
